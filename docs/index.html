Four questions no ordinary database can answer

1. What was true at any moment? Every fact carries its effective time. AS OF '2026-03-15' — or just YESTERDAY.
2. What did we believe at any moment? A second clock records when you learned each fact. AS KNOWN AT replays past decisions fairly.
3. Why did it change? Causes are stored as first-class links. WHY walks the chain. No war room required.
4. Can we trust it — and did it land? Provenance and confidence on every fact. PENDING finds changes that were sent but never applied.

CeQL — a query language for time, cause, and trust

-- what did the register believe the price was, last Tuesday at 2pm Central?
FACTS OF item:100001/store:4001 FACET register
  AS OF 'last week 2pm CST' AS KNOWN AT '2026-03-01' WHY

-- where do our systems disagree about price, right now?
DISAGREE ON price_cents

-- average per facet, only groups with enough data
FACTS facet, COUNT(*), AVG(price_cents) OF item:*
  GROUP BY facet HAVING COUNT(*) > 5

Or skip the syntax: the dashboard translates plain English — "price of item X yesterday at 2pm CST" — into CeQL, deterministically, no AI tokens. The full textbook includes a Rosetta Stone mapping every Oracle, DB2, PostgreSQL, MongoDB, Cypher, and KSQL idiom to CeQL.

Honest limits

What Centauri is not (yet): it's a single-node engine with read replicas — no clustering or sharding. Indexes live in memory, so your data must fit in RAM. There's no cost-based optimizer. Encryption at rest with crypto-erasure is on the roadmap. Centauri isn't a Postgres replacement — it's the flight recorder beside your operational stores: the system of record for what happened, when, why, and how much to trust it.
